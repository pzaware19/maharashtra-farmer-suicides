# Maharashtra's Farmer Suicide Crisis: Two Decades in Data

**By Piyush Zaware**
*Researcher, Global Poverty Research Laboratory, Northwestern Kellogg School of Management*

Between 2001 and 2022, 66,000 farmers killed themselves in Maharashtra. That works out to roughly eight farmers per day, every day, for two decades. India as a whole accounts for roughly a third of all farmer suicides recorded globally. Maharashtra, for most of those years, accounts for a third of India's.

These numbers come from the NCRB, the National Crime Records Bureau, which publishes cause-of-death data annually in its Accidental Deaths and Suicides in India series. The data has gaps and definitional problems, and some researchers think it undercounts. Nobody thinks it overcounts. What the numbers show, even conservatively, is a sustained catastrophe in one of India's richest states.

The question worth asking in 2026 is not whether the crisis exists. It does. The question is where it is, why it is there, whether anything tried over the past two decades has worked, and what a serious policy response would require.

---

## Not all of Maharashtra

The first thing the data shows clearly is that this is not a Maharashtra crisis. It is a Vidarbha crisis.

Vidarbha is the eastern chunk of Maharashtra, eleven districts running along the border with Chhattisgarh and Telangana. These eleven districts account for roughly half the state's farmer suicide total in most years. Six of them form the epicentre: Yavatmal, Amravati, Akola, Washim, Buldhana, Wardha. Yavatmal alone, a district of around 2.7 million people, regularly records more farmer suicides than most Indian states.

The rest of Maharashtra does not look like Vidarbha. Pune, Nashik, Kolhapur, the coastal Konkan belt show elevated rates compared to urban areas, but not dramatically different from other agricultural states. It is Vidarbha and, to a lesser extent, Marathwada to the southwest that push Maharashtra's totals to levels that make it a national outlier.

---

## Three regions, three crops, the same trap

Maharashtra is not a one-crop state, and the crisis is not a one-crop story.

Vidarbha grows cotton almost exclusively. Marathwada, the eight-district belt to the west, depends heavily on soybean. The Nashik plateau and Ahmednagar district grow nearly half of India's onions. Each region is exposed to a different commodity market, and each has seen that market crash in ways that translate directly into farm household distress.

The common structure is identical across all three crops. Farmers borrow at the start of the season to buy seed, fertiliser, and pesticide. They plant a single cash crop and wait for what the market offers at harvest. There is no food crop to eat if the market fails. There is no crop insurance that reliably pays out before creditors arrive. There is the debt, the harvest, and the price.

Cotton is the most documented case because Vidarbha is where the crisis is most concentrated and cotton prices are tracked by a global benchmark, the COTLOOK A index. Soybean prices in Marathwada follow global CBOT movements. Onion prices at the Lasalgaon mandi in Nashik, Asia's largest onion wholesale market, have swung between Rs 310 per quintal in 2010 and Rs 4,200 in 2019. In 2010, farmers in Nashik were burning onions by the roadside because the price at the mandi did not cover transport costs.

The crop changes by region. The trap does not.

## The timeline: waivers that work, then stop working

Maharashtra's farmer suicide numbers peaked in 2006 at 4,453. That year, P. Sainath's reporting in *The Hindu* brought Vidarbha to national attention. Prime Minister Manmohan Singh visited. A Rs 3,750 crore package targeted at Vidarbha's core districts followed. Then, in 2008, the UPA government announced a national farm loan waiver worth Rs 71,000 crore.

It worked, for a while. Suicides fell through 2009, reached a low of around 2,800, then climbed again through the early 2010s. By 2012, the count was back above 3,700.

A second waiver came in 2017, this one state-level, under Chief Minister Devendra Fadnavis, worth Rs 34,000 crore. Suicides fell again. In 2018, the total dropped to 2,239, the lowest in the data. Then they climbed. By 2021, the count was back above 2,700.

The pattern is consistent across both interventions: a loan waiver reduces suicides for two to three years, then the numbers recover. A state-level regression on the NCRB data confirms this: controlling for cotton prices and monsoon, the 2008 waiver window is associated with roughly 18 percent fewer suicides in the three years that followed, and the 2017 waiver with roughly 15 percent fewer. Both effects are in the expected direction. The 2017 estimate is statistically significant.

Debt relief is real relief. But a farmer whose current debt is cleared still has to borrow again next planting season. The waiver addresses the immediate crisis. The conditions that created it are untouched.

## Who dies

Male farmers account for roughly 90 percent of all recorded farmer suicides in Maharashtra across the entire period. That proportion has barely shifted in two decades. Female farmer suicides are smaller in absolute numbers but follow exactly the same pattern: rising toward the 2006 peak, falling after the 2008 waiver, climbing, falling after the 2017 waiver, climbing again.

The male concentration partly reflects who holds formal title to land and therefore who holds formal debt. Kisan credit cards, crop loans from nationalised banks, and informal moneylender loans in Vidarbha are predominantly in the male head-of-household's name. The debt, when it cannot be repaid, is most often that person's to carry. Female farmer suicides are not a rounding error in this data. They represent the same structural pressures, in households where the woman is formally recorded as the cultivator.

---

## Maharashtra in national context

Maharashtra has led India's farmer suicide count for most of the past two decades. But that lead needs to be read carefully.

In absolute numbers, Maharashtra is consistently first. Karnataka runs a close second, with a sharp spike in 2016 during a severe drought year. Andhra Pradesh and Telangana combined have fallen steadily since the mid-2000s. Madhya Pradesh dropped sharply after 2017, partly from loan waiver effects, partly from changes in NCRB's counting methodology.

On a per-capita basis, adjusting for the number of cultivators in each state, the picture is different. Maharashtra's lead narrows. Karnataka's per-capita rate has matched or exceeded Maharashtra's in several years, including 2016. Madhya Pradesh, which looks significant in absolute terms, nearly vanishes once you account for its large agricultural population.

The per-capita comparison matters because it shifts the question from "which state has the most suicides" to "which state has the highest risk per farmer." Both Karnataka and Maharashtra score high by that measure. The scale of the problem in Maharashtra reflects, in part, the scale of cotton-dependent farming in a single region.

---

## When prices crash

The cotton price signal is inverse and clear in the raw data: when the COTLOOK A index rises, Maharashtra suicides tend to fall; when it crashes, deaths follow. The most direct illustration is 2010 to 2012. Global cotton prices nearly tripled in the 2010-11 season after flooding in Pakistan cut world supply. Maharashtra's suicide count fell in both years. Then the boom ended. Prices collapsed by nearly half in 2012. By 2013, suicides were back above 3,700.

Soybean tells a parallel story in Marathwada. The World Bank benchmark price for soybeans dropped nearly 40 percent between 2012 and 2016. Indian soybean MSP rose every year over that period, but actual farm-gate prices in Marathwada are tied to the crushing industry's procurement price, which tracks global markets. When the world price fell, MSP provided partial cushion but did not cover cost of cultivation for many farmers.

The onion story is different in character, more violent in amplitude. Lasalgaon prices have moved by a factor of 14 between their lowest and highest points since 2001. Farmers who planted onions in 2010 expecting reasonable prices faced Rs 310 per quintal at harvest. Farmers who planted in 2019 received Rs 4,200. The price risk is not managed. It is absorbed entirely by whoever holds the crop at harvest.

## The monsoon as trigger, not cause

Below-normal rainfall is associated with higher farmer suicides, but the relationship is noisier than the narrative suggests. Two years illustrate why.

In 2006, Maharashtra recorded 4,453 farmer suicides: the highest in the data. Vidarbha had a near-normal monsoon that year. Bt cotton had just been widely adopted across the region, input costs had jumped, and credit markets were tight. The cause was structural, not climatic.

In 2009, Vidarbha experienced a monsoon deficit of more than 20 percent, well past the drought threshold. Suicides fell. The 2008 loan waiver had just cleared outstanding debt across the region. When the monsoon failed, the debt burden had already been lifted.

What the data shows is that a bad monsoon accelerates a debt crisis that is already in progress. It is a trigger, not the underlying cause. Farmers who are heavily indebted, farming a single cash crop with no fallback, and priced into a market they cannot hedge against are vulnerable. A drought makes the season in which the crisis tips over more likely. It does not create the underlying vulnerability.

---

## What a simple regression shows

A state-level regression on 22 years of NCRB data puts numbers on these relationships. Three variables, jointly, explain 84 percent of the year-to-year variation in Maharashtra farmer suicides: the COTLOOK A cotton price index, Vidarbha monsoon rainfall, and binary indicators for the two loan waiver windows.

The waiver effects are the most precisely estimated. The 2008 national waiver is associated with roughly 18 percent fewer suicides in the following three years. The 2017 Maharashtra waiver is associated with 15 percent fewer. Both are in the expected direction. The 2017 estimate is statistically significant at the 5 percent level.

The cotton price and rainfall coefficients are statistically noisy at n = 22. The reason is not that the relationships are spurious: the scatter plots show them clearly. The problem is that the 2010-11 cotton boom and the 2008 waiver window overlap almost exactly in time. The model cannot separate the two from state-level time series alone.

This is the methodological wall that a 22-year state series hits. The next step, which has not been done, requires district-year variation in prices, crop exposure, and insurance coverage.

---

## Why MSP has not solved it

The Minimum Support Price for cotton has risen roughly fourfold since 2001, from Rs 1,500 per quintal to Rs 6,080. On paper, that looks like substantial support. The data on cost of cultivation shows why it has not been enough.

The A2 cost of growing cotton in Vidarbha, covering paid-out expenses for seed, fertiliser, pesticide, hired labour, and machine hire, has risen from roughly Rs 2,300 per quintal in 2001 to around Rs 8,200 per quintal in 2022, based on CACP cost of cultivation surveys for Maharashtra. At typical Vidarbha yields of six to seven quintals per hectare on dryland farms, a farmer selling at full MSP in 2022 still recovers only about 74 percent of paid-out costs per quintal. In 2001, the ratio was similarly unfavourable.

MSP has never covered A2 cost in Vidarbha. It has not gotten closer. The Swaminathan Commission's recommendation of MSP at 1.5 times C2 cost, which adds imputed family labour and land rent to the A2 figure, would require roughly doubling the current MSP for cotton. That recommendation was made in 2006. It has not been implemented.

The problem is compounded by procurement. Cotton is covered under MSP, but the Cotton Corporation of India's actual procurement in Vidarbha has been unreliable in most years. CCI procurement centres are not uniformly accessible. Farmers without transport or storage cannot hold their cotton for government buyers. In years when gin mill prices fall below MSP and CCI doesn't step in adequately, the MSP is a number that exists on a government press release and not in a farmer's bank account.

The input cost side reinforces the squeeze. Diesel prices have risen 3.5-fold since 2001. DAP fertiliser, one of the most critical nutrient inputs for cotton and soybean, has risen nearly threefold, with a sharp spike during the 2008 global phosphate price shock. Together, diesel and DAP account for 40 to 50 percent of the A2 cost of cotton cultivation. When both rise faster than MSP, the space between what a crop costs and what it fetches narrows further.

## What a serious evaluation would need

India has tried loan waivers twice at scale. It has tried crop insurance through PMFBY, launched in 2016 with premium subsidies and expanded coverage. It has tried MSP procurement through CCI. What it has not done is a rigorous evaluation of which of these interventions actually reduces suicides, and by how much, and under what conditions.

The data to do this exists. The Maharashtra government holds district-level records on farmer suicides, PMFBY enrolment and claims, CCI procurement volumes, kisan credit card disbursements, and IMD rainfall. The variation in PMFBY take-up across districts and years, driven partly by local outreach and insurer presence, creates the kind of quasi-experimental variation that identifies a causal effect.

A difference-in-differences study comparing districts where PMFBY coverage jumped sharply in a given year to comparable districts where it did not would tell you whether insurance reduces suicides, by how much, and whether claims settlement speed matters more than coverage alone. A similar design around CCI procurement volumes would show whether actual procurement in a district reduces suicides in that district that year.

These questions have not been answered in a published study or a government evaluation report. The data sits in separate systems across NCRB, the PMFBY portal, CCI, and IMD. Nobody has pulled it together and run the analysis. There is also limited political appetite to fund an evaluation whose answer might be "the scheme is not reducing suicides by much."

Sixty-six thousand deaths over twenty-two years is enough data. It should not require more.

---

## Two decades on

The crisis in Vidarbha is not new. Sainath wrote about it twenty years ago. Prime ministers visited. Resources flowed. The numbers fell, then recovered, fell again, recovered again.

What is different in 2026 is that the data infrastructure to study this rigorously now exists. Two decades of NCRB district-level records. IMD rainfall at fine geographic resolution. State-level disbursement data from PMFBY and CCI. The tools to do a credible evaluation are available. The evaluation has not been done.

The structural problem is not hard to describe: cash-crop monoculture, borrowed capital, no hedge against price and weather risk, an MSP that never covered costs, and crop insurance that does not pay fast enough to matter when creditors arrive. Loan waivers interrupt the cycle temporarily. None of the permanent interventions have been rigorously tested against the outcome they are supposed to affect.

That is the gap. Not in compassion or policy effort. In evidence.
